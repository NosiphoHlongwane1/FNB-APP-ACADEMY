Koala

The koala (Phascolarctos cinereus, or, inaccurately, koala bear) is an arboreal herbivorous marsupial native to Australia. It is the only extant representative of the family Phascolarctidae and its closest living relatives are the wombats.

Found in coastal areas of eastern and southern Australia, koalas inhabit Queensland, New South Wales, Victoria, and South Australia.

Koalas are known for their stout, tailless body, large head with round fluffy ears, and spoon-shaped nose. Their fur ranges from silver grey to chocolate brown.

They typically inhabit open eucalypt woodlands and feed mainly on eucalyptus leaves. Due to their low-energy diet, koalas sleep up to 20 hours a day.

These mostly asocial animals bond only between mothers and their young. Male koalas produce loud bellows to attract mates and assert dominance.

Their young, called joeys, develop in the mother’s pouch and are fully weaned by one year. Koalas face threats from disease, drought, and bushfires.
