Giraffes

Giraffes (Giraffa camelopardalis) are the tallest land animals on Earth, known for their extraordinarily long necks and legs. Native to African savannas, they can reach heights up to 5.5 meters (18 feet).

Their distinctive coat patterns of brown patches separated by lighter lines help with camouflage in the wild. Each giraffe's pattern is unique, much like a human fingerprint.

Giraffes use their long necks to browse treetop leaves, especially acacia, and their tongues can extend up to 45 cm to grab food. Despite their size, they only need to drink water every few days.

Social and gentle, giraffes live in loosely structured herds and communicate through subtle movements and low-frequency sounds.
